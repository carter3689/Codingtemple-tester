About Me

Hello! My name is Christie Ziegler. I come from a background in biology and I became interested in software development while working on a bioinformatics module at my last job. I'm also an artist and web development seems to be a good chance to integrate my interest in art and programming.

My software development background

Last year I worked at the Field Museum, developing a bioinformatics tool for Python for working with evolutionary trees. The module I worked on allows scientists to load in a phylogenetic tree and plot it interactively. It also allows scientists to perform analyses on phylogenetic trees to learn more about how that group evolved. If you are interested in learning more, check out the documentation!

I enjoyed working on this project a lot, but unfortunately the funding ran out, so now I am looking for more practical experience so I can get a job doing full-stack web development. I am already familiar with Python so I am excited to learn Django. My ideal job would be working for a startup or other small company.

I enjoy programming because I like problem-solving. Taking a task and deciding how to break it down and solve it is like solving a giant puzzle, and puzzles are fun! And there's no better feeling than finally finishing a large project after days or weeks of work. You feel like you're on top of the world, at least until the next problem crops up!

My art background

I have been drawing as a hobby for over ten years. I am familiar with Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator, so I hope these experiences will help me when designing websites.

I mostly draw animals. They're interesting and it's always a challenge to learn to draw a new animal. I usually practice realism. Some people think realism is extremely challenging, but it's actually fairly straightforward if you use a reference photo. Even better if you took the reference photo yourself! When drawing realism, it's important not to get ahead of yourself and focus on rendering minute details. Beautifully rendered fur on a fox's face isn't going to count for much if the structure of the animal's head looks really weird! It's important to focus on large shapes. Make sure your areas of light and shadow are formed properly and the rest will fall into place. If all else fails, just look at your reference photo!

I also like to draw cartoons. I find stylized art to be more difficult than realism because you have to bring it upon yourself to decide how the image should look. While it's always good to use a reference image, you have to personally decide how you want to stylize the image. It can be difficult to decide what to do when your image doesn't look right when creating a stylized picture, but as always, practice makes perfect!
